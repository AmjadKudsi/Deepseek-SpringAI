### **Derivative of \( x^2 \)**  
The derivative of \( x^2 \) with respect to \( x \) is:  
\[
\frac{d}{dx}(x^2) = 2x
\]  

---

### **Why?**  
The derivative tells us how a function changes at any point. For \( x^2 \), we can find the derivative using the **power rule**:  

**Power Rule:**  
If \( f(x) = x^n \), then its derivative is \( f'(x) = n \cdot x^{n-1} \).  

Applying this to \( x^2 \):  
1. The exponent (\( n \)) is **2**.  
2. Multiply by the exponent: \( 2 \cdot x^{2-1} = 2x^1 = 2x \).  

---

### **Intuitive Explanation**  
Imagine \( x^2 \) represents the area of a square with side length \( x \).  

- If you increase \( x \) slightly, how much does the area (\( x^2 \)) change?  
- The derivative (\( 2x \)) says: "For a tiny increase in \( x \), the area grows at a rate of \( 2x \)."  

*(Example: When \( x = 3 \), the derivative \( 2(3) = 6 \) means the area grows 6 times as fast as the side length at that point.)*  

---
